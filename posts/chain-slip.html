A fix for a slipping chain

It's been nearly two years since I Bafanged¹ my very old (circa early 1970's) Raleigh road bike, and it has honestly been good as gold. The sum of the maintenance over the past two years has been:

  • A set of tyres²
  • A set of brake pads
  • A new rim³

Really not bad for a bike from the 70's that is ridden at least 15km nearly every day. Most of that is down to the fact that bicycles are incredibly simple things, with not all that much to go wrong.

So with that said, I recently went for a spin into the hills of Howth, which are very well known to lycra-wearing Dubliners, and much to my embarrassment, found that my chain was slipping pretty severely on the hills. This is not ideal when overtaking Mamils on the hills.

Diagnosis

Initially, I suspected an internal issue with the Bafang motor, which has a nylon drive gear, since it didn't seem like the external chain was slipping. However, I disproved this by applying a lot of torque to the pedals myself, reproducing the issue without using the motor.

Next, I checked the chain, and found that it was surprisingly loose. The rear derailleur is responsible for tensioning the chain.

In my case, the pulley cage was partially seized, and the tension from the spring was not sufficient for the t-pulley to tension the chain.

The Fix

A healthy dose of WD-40 and a good clean with a micro-fibre cloth to clean out the area did the trick. I then worked the pulley cage over and back for about a minute and then applied a small amount of chain lube at the pivot point to help reduce friction. A nice easy fix.

Root Cause

So why did this actually happen? The answer is most likely poor maintenance on my part. I have actually been relatively consistent at lubricating the chain on this bike. I use wet lube, which is designed for wet weather conditions⁴ which means that it won't get washed away when riding in the wet, but it also means that it attracts much more dirt and grime to the chain. I have also in the past been guilty of (1) not sufficiently cleaning down the chain between applications, and (2) applying too much lube.

This combination of errors on my part likely caused dirt to build up in the pivot point and prevent the pulley case from moving, preventing the chain from being tensioned and causing embarrassment for me on the hills. Serves me right, lesson learned.
